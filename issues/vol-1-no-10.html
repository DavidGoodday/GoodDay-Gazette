The Word From the Top

"We're Officially in the Holiday Rush": Hency Calls for Sober Timelines as Peak Season Opens

A company-wide reset on how GoodDay quotes implementation dates — driven, again, by the WMS bottleneck — lands the same week a weekend reconciliation outage swept 43 merchants.
By the Gazette Desk · Reporting July 3 – 10, 2026

Kyle Hency opened the week with a message to #go-to-market that reframed how the whole company should talk about time. New sales closing now sits four weeks out — the first week of August — "which means we're officially in the holiday rush." Brands need to feel the urgency of starting by mid-August to safely finish before the holidays, he wrote, and reps must lead with caution, not just creativity.

The culprit, stated plainly: WMS. "When we review the delayed and missed expectations in our processes, they're most often WMS related. Current flavor with DCL, Stord, Moda." The instruction going forward — for any brand on a new WMS, add two weeks by default and share the risk up front. The same goes for integrations not yet built: be clear on what is and isn't shipping, and be more cautious about delivery windows.

Alongside the memo came a preliminary July–September roadmap for the team's reference, with a pointed request that SOWs and Blueprints line up with it — "including some conservatism" — and that a "New GoodDay Features" section be added so risk gets flagged earlier. It is less a retreat than a recalibration: set expectations honestly, then keep solving hard problems.

The timing was almost too on-the-nose. As Hency asked for humility about delivery, the platform spent the weekend proving his point.

Evan Smith works to size the Joor opportunity while flagging a two-way item-sync gap as a "major blocker" across seven partners.

The Revenue Ledger Grows Up — and Absolute Publishing Gets Its Guardrails

Skills, Agents & the MCP

The Counterintuitive Rollout Lesson: Share Fewer Skills, Not More

Adoption climbs when GoodDay stops handing merchants default skills up front — plus Dave specs a Product Delivery Agent to close the build-vs-deliver gap.
Sources: #project-ai, #mcp-launch-skills

The week's sharpest insight came from Harish: MCP adoption has been stronger when GoodDay doesn't push default skills at onboarding. "When we do, people explore less, and it's starting to create confusion." His approach now — hold off on canned skills, and instead build one live with the merchant, starting from a pain point they describe. Dave loved it: "a live skill / artifact design session." The proof is landing: Matt Pham delivered a split-and-reassign tool to Lola ("Lola is happy") and already filed a ticket for the MCP to read and write their custom fields.

Demand is broadening. Yardsale is being teed up for MCP access, Hey Gang is looking at building into GoodDay for a B2B shopping experience (River shipped a quick AI one-pager to explain the concept, careful to note today's access is read-only), Seniq has outgrown Claude Artifacts and is moving toward Sigma BI, and Sarah Flint wants an AI Inventory Planning demo. A brief July 6 outage — Claude itself was down, and Mini MCP needed a reconnect — was the week's only stumble.

The bigger swing is internal. Dave circulated a spec for a Product Delivery Agent: a living roadmap app plus a weekly delivery update, aggregated from Linear, the monorepo, PM plans, Slack and Granola notes, assessing each project against its own definition of "delivered into X merchants' hands, stable for Y weeks." Agent-drafted, human-reviewed, nothing auto-posts — built on the same skill patterns as goodday-pm and Ser Rekonalot, with zero engineering time.
