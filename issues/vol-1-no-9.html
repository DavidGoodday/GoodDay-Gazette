v0.135.0 Ships The Fractional Platform — And Nearly Runs Prod Out Of Disk

The six-decimal upgrade goes to production as a major release, but hand-run online migrations balloon the database until Cloud SQL shuts the prod primary down to save itself.
By the Engineering Desk · Filed from #engineering, #critical-support, and #sers-tavern

On Monday, June 29, Troy Lowe cut and released v0.135.0 — the system-wide decimal quantity precision upgrade the team had spent the prior two weeks building. Fractional units now run end-to-end across inventory, orders, fulfillment, receipts, transfers, kits, and snapshots, alongside Units-of-Measure tracking, a new public Items API, a new partners table, and Observability Slices 1–5. Crucially, Troy tagged the three heaviest 6DP migrations to not auto-run on the deploy — they would be migrated by hand, online, during low-traffic windows. That decision defined the rest of the week.

The online migration of the three largest tables — ledger_entries, inventory line items, and adjustments — became a multi-day grind. Troy ran them in the background from June 30 ("~5 hours to go" on ledger_entries alone) and asked the team to freeze hotfix migrations to avoid overlap. The reliability low point came Tuesday, July 1, at ~16:31, when Vishal Marocha caught a Cloud SQL alert: the prod database had shut itself down to prevent running out of disk, needing at least 2,200 GB. Noel confirmed resolution by ~17:05 after raising the DB size. By July 3, Troy reported "8 times is the charm" — ledger_entries had finally migrated.

It did not ship alone. Ten prod deploys landed (v0.134.15–.18 Friday, then v0.135.0–.5). Separately, Shopify reconciliation had silently stopped running for Lola Blankets (GD-11898) — Noel traced it to a keeper-dedupe query timing out against 754,776 bloated integration_object rows; Sai Ancha cleared ~700K rows and re-triggered recon. Pylon volume eased to 174 tickets. Full coverage on Pages 4–6.

Duvin's Loop go-live turns to hardening, PO-vs-Lot costing becomes Paka's #1 ask, and reconciliation starts reversing manual transfers.
Sourced from 10 project Slack channels

Returns Management dominated the traffic as the team hardened the fresh Duvin/Loop go-live. Dave (wardeez) ran a punch-list of questions against the Loop connection — how flags travel from the WMS up to Loop, why Duvin's returns were auto-closing (Noel traced it to Loop closing on EasyPost delivery events, and proposed keeping the GoodDay return open until label status resolves), missing return-reason pass-through to ShipHero, and credit-memo line items that weren't populating. Noel recorded a happy-path walkthrough, and the team decided to "show and tell" Duvin's real instance in Tuesday's webinar rather than risk a live demo across the Loop→Trackstar→GoodDay lag. Credit Memos with line items and QBO sync moved toward the near-term plan.

The second thread was accounting and costing pressure from real merchants. Bogey Bros asked for PO-invoice push to QuickBooks; a Macroscope code dig confirmed the only existing path is the two-year-old Tangerine one-off (posts transformed PO data to a Zapier webhook, first PO block only) and that it overlaps heavily with the planned 3-way-match agent. Kyle flagged it as a recurring sales-cycle ask (Pittsboro Feed churned partly over it; BeamRoom wants it). Meanwhile the PO-vs-Lot-Costing report became "the #1 thing we need to deliver for Paka," with Dave calling to halt MCP work and pull in Vishal to crack it.

The MCP Grows Up: A Ticketed Intake And A Sellable-Stock Bug

Requests pile up as Slack messages, Matt Pham finds the MCP double-counting committed orders, and the team wires Slack → Pylon → Linear.

The AI Desk this week was less about new model capability and more about the operational reality of running an MCP product with real customers filing real requests. Harish Chandramowli is drowning in inbound: MCP polish and feature asks are arriving as Slack messages, and he opened a "101 customer support" question — how do you turn a Slack request into a Pylon ticket? Matt Pham owned the gap, admitting "that's on me — I need to be making Linear tickets for you versus slacking you," and committing to a fix. Harish set himself a two-day goal to clear the backlog for a Monday release and asked the Linear bot to sweep last week's Slack tickets into the MCP project under his name.

The concrete engineering find of the week was a data-integrity bug in the MCP itself: Matt Pham discovered the MCP's available-to-sell calculation is subtracting committed orders twice, so it reports less sellable inventory than actually exists. He filed GD-11850 against Bogey Bros and assigned it to Harish. Alongside it, an adjustment-API gap got queued for a ticket, and Sarah's reporting issue prompted Harish to weigh building a Sigma report rather than "fight artifacts."

Process envy also crept in: Harish praised Linear's in-thread approval workflow ("something to copy when we have Slack bots") while venting about a month-long approval he still can't get pushed through. The throughline is a team maturing from ad-hoc Slack support toward a real ticketed intake pipeline — Pylon for inbound capture, Linear for MCP project tracking — as MCP usage scales.
